Clarkson sits in the southwest corner of Mississauga, along the Lake Ontario
shoreline, and is one of the oldest settled communities in the city. Unlike
the newer suburban stretches further north, Clarkson still has a distinct
village core and a genuine 19th-century history behind it, not just a
retrofitted name.

## A short history

The community traces back to 1808, when fifteen-year-old Warren Clarkson and
his brother Joshua left Albany, New York for land near Lake Ontario. Warren
stayed, and in 1835 opened a general store on what is now Clarkson Road
North, a site that became known locally as Clarkson's Corner. A railway
station followed in 1853, built by the Great Western Railway near that same
corner.

Clarkson's other defining chapter is agricultural. Strawberry and raspberry
cultivation were introduced to the area in the 1850s, and by the early
1900s Clarkson had become known as the "Strawberry Capital of Ontario." A
sign at the Clarkson railway station in 1915 reportedly declared that more
strawberries passed through it than any other station in the province.
Commercial fruit farming continued in the area well into the 20th century
before the land gradually gave way to residential development.

## Getting there

**Clarkson GO Station** sits on GO Transit's **Lakeshore West line**, which
runs frequent, all-day service rather than the weekday-rush-hour-only
pattern you'll find on the Milton line through Streetsville. That makes
Clarkson one of the more transit-convenient older neighbourhoods in the
city for getting to and from Toronto. MiWay buses connect it to the rest of
Mississauga. See our [transit guide](/guides/getting-around-mississauga-transit/)
for the fuller picture.

## What's there

- **Clarkson Village.** The historic core along Lakeshore Road and Clarkson
  Road, with independent shops, cafés, and restaurants. Browse our
  [restaurant](/directory/restaurants/) and [café & bakery](/directory/cafes/)
  directories for what's currently open nearby.
- **Benares Historic House.** An 1857 estate house (with sections believed
  to date to around 1835) that belonged to the Harris family for
  generations before being donated to the Ontario Heritage Foundation in
  1968. It opened to the public as a museum in 1995, fully furnished with
  original Harris family items.
- **Bradley Museum.** Centred on a farmhouse built in 1830 by Lewis and
  Elizabeth Bradley, United Empire Loyalists who raised seven children on
  the property. Together with Benares, it's one of the clearest windows
  into early Mississauga life still standing.
- **The Lake Ontario waterfront.** Clarkson's shoreline connects into the
  wider Waterfront Trail system that also runs through Port Credit further
  east; see our [Port Credit guide](/guides/port-credit-neighbourhood-guide/)
  for that stretch.

## Good to know

- Clarkson is sometimes used loosely to describe a wider area than the
  historic village itself, so double-check an address if you're expecting
  walking distance to the village core.
- Both Benares Historic House and Bradley Museum are seasonal and
  program-dependent operations, so hours and admission can change. Check
  our [Things to Do](/events/) page for current listings before visiting,
  rather than relying on a fixed schedule.
