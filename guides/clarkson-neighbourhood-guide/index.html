Clarkson: A Neighbourhood Guide

Published July 19, 2026 · Mississauga Insider

Clarkson sits in the southwest corner of Mississauga, along the Lake Ontario shoreline, and is one of the oldest settled communities in the city. Unlike the newer suburban stretches further north, Clarkson still has a distinct village core and a genuine 19th-century history behind it, not just a retrofitted name.

A short history

The community traces back to 1808, when fifteen-year-old Warren Clarkson and his brother Joshua left Albany, New York for land near Lake Ontario. Warren stayed, and in 1835 opened a general store on what is now Clarkson Road North, a site that became known locally as Clarkson’s Corner. A railway station followed in 1853, built by the Great Western Railway near that same corner.

Clarkson’s other defining chapter is agricultural. Strawberry and raspberry cultivation were introduced to the area in the 1850s, and by the early 1900s Clarkson had become known as the “Strawberry Capital of Ontario.” A sign at the Clarkson railway station in 1915 reportedly declared that more strawberries passed through it than any other station in the province. Commercial fruit farming continued in the area well into the 20th century before the land gradually gave way to residential development.

Getting there

Clarkson GO Station sits on GO Transit’s Lakeshore West line, which runs frequent, all-day service rather than the weekday-rush-hour-only pattern you’ll find on the Milton line through Streetsville. That makes Clarkson one of the more transit-convenient older neighbourhoods in the city for getting to and from Toronto. MiWay buses connect it to the rest of Mississauga. See our transit guide for the fuller picture.

What’s there

  • Clarkson Village. The historic core along Lakeshore Road and Clarkson Road, with independent shops, cafés, and restaurants. Browse our restaurant and café & bakery directories for what’s currently open nearby.
  • Benares Historic House. An 1857 estate house (with sections and outbuildings dating to 1837) that belonged to four generations of the Harris and Sayers families before being donated to the Ontario Heritage Trust in 1968. It opened to the public as a museum in 1995, with more than 95% of the artifacts on display original to the family and the home.
  • Bradley Museum. Centred on a farmhouse built in 1830 by Lewis and Elizabeth Bradley, United Empire Loyalists who raised seven children on the property. Together with Benares, it’s one of the clearest windows into early Mississauga life still standing.
  • The Lake Ontario waterfront. Clarkson’s shoreline connects into the wider Waterfront Trail system that also runs through Port Credit further east; see our Port Credit guide for that stretch.

Good to know

  • Clarkson is sometimes used loosely to describe a wider area than the historic village itself, so double-check an address if you’re expecting walking distance to the village core.
  • Both Benares Historic House and Bradley Museum are seasonal and program-dependent operations, so hours and admission can change. Check our Things to Do page for current listings before visiting, rather than relying on a fixed schedule.
